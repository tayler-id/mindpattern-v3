# Framework Catalog

## Core decision rule
Pick the framework based on the job:
- clarity cleanup: Orwell, Zinsser, Flesch, Williams, Pinker
- oral force and speech-like prose: Hitchens, Noonan, Jobs
- executive logic and memo architecture: Minto, Bezos, Grove, Drucker
- formal argument: Toulmin
- bridge-building on contentious topics: Rogerian
- public-facing editorial and op-ed work: hook, nut-graf, and op-ed patterns

## Quick summaries

### Orwell
Best for subtraction, plain language, anti-jargon, and active prose. Core rules: never use a metaphor you've seen in print, prefer short words, cut any word you can, use active voice, avoid foreign or scientific words when an everyday equivalent exists.

### Zinsser
Best for nonfiction clarity and warmth. Strip every sentence to its cleanest components. Rewrite ruthlessly. Unity of pronoun, tense, and mood matters.

### Flesch
Best for readability scoring and simplification. Short sentences, short words, personal references. Use when the audience is broad or low-context.

### Williams
Best for diagnosing why prose feels dense. Fix nominalization (zombie nouns), front-load subjects and verbs, keep old-before-new information flow.

### Pinker
Best for explaining complex ideas clearly. Avoid the curse of knowledge. Use concrete language and classic style (writer as guide, reader as equal).

### Hitchens
Best for prose that must sound alive when spoken aloud. Read-aloud testing matters. Combine erudition with plain force.

### Noonan
Best for speech-like cadence and emotional resonance. Short declarative sentences, concrete imagery, and earned climaxes. Pair with Hitchens for rhetorical force.

### Minto
Best for decision documents. Top-down communication, SCQA openings, and MECE grouping.

### Bezos
Best for memo discipline and narrative reasoning. Six-page narrative memos, no slides. Forces the writer to think through the logic fully.

### Grove
Best for operational decision memos. Clear problem statement, options with tradeoffs, and a recommended action.

### Drucker
Best for management communication. Focus on contribution, results, and what the reader needs to decide or do next.

### Jobs
Best when short dramatic persuasion with a rule-of-three structure.

### Toulmin
Best when the user needs a defensible claim with warrants and rebuttal handling.

### Rogerian
Best when the audience is resistant and the goal is movement rather than combat.

### Nut-graf and op-ed structure
Best when the piece needs a strong public-facing arc with hook, setup, diagnosis, prescription, concession, and close.

## Selection cheatsheet
- "Make this clearer" means Orwell plus Williams
- "Help me write a leadership memo" means Minto plus Bezos
- "Make this sound stronger aloud" means Hitchens plus Noonan
- "Help me argue this rigorously" means Toulmin
- "Help me persuade skeptics" means Rogerian
- "Help me write an op-ed" means op-ed structure, possibly supported by Toulmin
